What's RightsCon

Not only is RightsCon 2025 being held in East Asia for the first time, but it has also chosen Taipei (臺北) as its hub. It is expected that approximately 3,000 experts from industry, government, and academia, representing 150 countries, will visit Taiwan in person, featuring over 500 sessions across 18 tracks to discuss topics such as artificial intelligence, digital governance, open-source applications in civic advocacy, and resistance to digital oppression. It is an excellent opportunity for Taiwan’s open-source technology community and human rights organizations to get involved global digital rights issues!

  • Date: 2025/2/24 (Mon) - 2025/2/27 (Thu) UTC+8
  • Location: Taipei International Convention Center (TICC) (台北國際會議中心)
  • Participation: Hybrid (Online and Onsite)
  • Main Language: English (with some sessions offering real-time Mandarin interpretation)
  • Official Website: https://www.rightscon.org

✔ Open Parliament ✔ Digital Identity and Privacy Autonomy ✔ Digital Gender Violence Issues

This event aims to present Taiwan and East Asia’s digital rights landscape, highlighting current challenges faced by digital rights defenders and fostering resonance with participants' experiences.

Organized by the Open Culture Foundation in collaboration with local partners - Odditysay Labs, Citizen Congress Watch and FLAME. This event is open to all RightsCon participants, a half-day series of events will be co-created, to be held on Monday, February 24, from 09:00 to 12:30. (UTC+8)

OCF's Session in RightsCon

Citizens for Citizens: Digital Security Support in Taiwan from Individual to Regional Project

Dialogue (In-Person)

February 26, 14:00–15:00 (UTC+8)

Room 101C

Weichem Lim/Open Culture Foundation
Toomore Chiang/Open Culture Foundation
Pellaeon Lin/Citizen Lab
Li-Yi Shao/TechSoup Global
Ddio Chuang

Human rights defenders in East Asia face growing digital threats, increasing the demand for security support. How can we engage more tech-savvy talents in this work? Using Taiwan as a case study, this dialogue brings together four citizen representatives to explore solutions. An April 2024 report by the Open Culture Foundation (Open Culture Foundation) found that half of 35 surveyed human rights groups faced data leaks, hacking, or platform shutdowns, often from authoritarian actors. With limited technical skills and manpower, many rely on volunteers.

Despite strong citizen support, sustaining long-term resources remains a challenge. This session gathers regional and local representatives to discuss challenges, opportunities, and the resources needed. It features two segments: first, speakers share their experiences; and second, an interactive discussion opens with key questions, encouraging both global and local participants to explore ways to involve citizens in digital security efforts.
